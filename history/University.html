ANCIENT UNIVERSIY OF INDIA

1. Takshashila (Taxila)

Takshashila, also known as Taxila, was an ancient center of learning located in present-day Pakistan, dating back to at least the 5th century BCE. Renowned as one of the earliest universities in the world, it attracted students from across India and beyond, including regions like Persia, Greece, and China. The university offered a wide range of subjects, including Vedas, grammar, philosophy, medicine, surgery, archery, politics, warfare, astronomy, and accounts. Eminent teachers such as Chanakya, the advisor to Emperor Chandragupta Maurya, and the famous grammarian Panini were associated with Takshashila. Its decline began around the 5th century CE due to invasions and changing political landscapes, but its legacy as a premier ancient educational institution endures in history.

2. nalanda

Nalanda, located in present-day Bihar, India, was one of the most prestigious and renowned centers of learning in ancient times, flourishing from the 5th century CE to the 12th century CE. Established under the Gupta Empire, Nalanda attracted scholars and students from various parts of Asia, including China, Korea, Japan, Tibet, Mongolia, and Central Asia. It offered a wide range of studies, including philosophy, logic, grammar, medicine, astronomy, and Buddhism. The university was equipped with extensive libraries, known as "Dharmaganja," and housed thousands of students and teachers. Its decline began with the invasion of the Turkic Muslim army led by Bakhtiyar Khilji in the late 12th century, which led to its destruction and the loss of invaluable knowledge..

3. vikramshila

Vikramashila was an ancient university established in the late 8th or early 9th century by King Dharmapala of the Pala dynasty, located in present-day Bihar, India. It was renowned for its excellence in Buddhist studies, attracting scholars from across Asia. Vikramashila was one of the two most important centers of learning in India during its time, alongside Nalanda. The university had a structured curriculum that included subjects like philosophy, logic, grammar, metaphysics, and tantra. It featured a large monastery, a library, and six gates, each overseen by a scholar responsible for teaching and administration. Vikramashila's influence declined after it was destroyed by the forces of Bakhtiyar Khilji in the late 12th century.
